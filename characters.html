People and Powers

Characters

Track how each figure pressures Gilgamesh to change his understanding of strength, love, responsibility, and mortality.

Gilgamesh

King of Uruk, two-thirds divine and one-third human. At the beginning of the epic, he is powerful, restless, and tyrannical. He exhausts his people and treats his power as permission. His friendship with Enkidu changes him, and Enkidu's death forces him to confront the limit he cannot conquer: mortality. Gilgamesh matters because his movement from arrogance to grief to hard-earned wisdom gives the epic its emotional arc.

Enkidu

Created by the gods as Gilgamesh's equal and counterweight. Enkidu begins in the wilderness, living with animals, and becomes human through contact with Shamhat, food, clothing, language, and friendship. He is both Gilgamesh's double and his opposite. His death transforms the epic from heroic adventure into a meditation on grief and death.

Shamhat

The temple woman who brings Enkidu into human society. Her role is sometimes misunderstood as merely seductive, but she represents civilization: speech, sexuality, food, clothing, and the social world. Through Shamhat, Enkidu gains human consciousness and loses his original closeness to the animals.

Ninsun

Gilgamesh's mother, a wise goddess who interprets dreams and blesses the journey to the Cedar Forest. Ninsun helps students see that counsel, ritual, and family bonds matter even in a heroic story.

Shamash

The sun god and protector of Gilgamesh. Shamash supports the heroes in the Cedar Forest, but divine support does not erase moral consequence. His presence raises questions about justice, power, and divine approval.

Humbaba

Guardian of the Cedar Forest. Humbaba is monstrous and terrifying, yet he is also a guardian appointed by the gods. The decision to kill him is one of the epic's morally complicated moments. Students should ask whether Humbaba is only an obstacle or also a sign that heroic fame can become destructive.

Ishtar

The goddess who proposes marriage to Gilgamesh after the heroes return from the Cedar Forest. Gilgamesh rejects her harshly, and Ishtar responds by sending the Bull of Heaven. Her episode reveals the dangers of pride, insult, divine power, and gendered conflict.

The Bull of Heaven

A divine punishment sent after Gilgamesh insults Ishtar. The heroes defeat it, but victory does not protect them from consequence. The death sentence that follows falls on Enkidu.

Siduri

A tavern keeper at the edge of the world. She advises Gilgamesh to accept ordinary human life instead of chasing immortality. Her counsel is one of the clearest statements of the epic's wisdom: life is mortal, so human beings should honor food, clothing, work, love, and daily joy.

Urshanabi

The boatman who helps Gilgamesh cross the Waters of Death to reach Utnapishtim. He becomes part of Gilgamesh's return journey and helps guide him back toward Uruk.

Utnapishtim

The survivor of the great Flood, granted immortality by the gods. Gilgamesh seeks him because he wants to escape death. Utnapishtim tells the Flood story and tests Gilgamesh, showing him that immortality is not available through force or desire.

Utnapishtim's Wife

A quiet but important figure who asks Utnapishtim to show compassion to Gilgamesh. Her presence softens the episode and leads to the plant of rejuvenation.

Ea

The clever god who helps preserve human life during the Flood. Ea's role reveals conflict among the gods and raises questions about divine justice.

Enlil

The god associated with the decision to destroy humanity in the Flood. He later grants immortality to Utnapishtim and his wife after the Flood has passed.

Aruru

The creator goddess who forms Enkidu. Her act begins the central relationship of the epic.

The People and Elders of Uruk

The community affected by Gilgamesh's rule. They remind readers that kingship is not only personal glory; it is responsibility to a city and its people.
