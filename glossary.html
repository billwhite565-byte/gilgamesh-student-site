Glossary of Terms

Use this glossary as a reference while reading Stephen Mitchell's Gilgamesh. Some entries define characters or places; others explain ideas that appear again and again across the epic.

Anu
The sky god and one of the highest divine authorities in the epic. Ishtar appeals to Anu when she wants the Bull of Heaven sent against Gilgamesh and Enkidu.
Aruru
The creator goddess who forms Enkidu from clay. Her act answers the people's cry for relief from Gilgamesh's oppressive rule.
Bull of Heaven
A divine creature sent as punishment after Gilgamesh insults Ishtar. Gilgamesh and Enkidu defeat it, but the victory leads toward divine judgment and Enkidu's death.
Cedar Forest
A sacred forest guarded by Humbaba. The journey there gives Gilgamesh and Enkidu fame, but it also raises moral questions about violence, pride, and crossing sacred boundaries.
Civilization
The human world of cities, language, food, clothing, ritual, labor, law, and friendship. Enkidu's movement from the wilderness into civilization is one of the epic's central transformations.
Clay
The material from which Aruru creates Enkidu. Clay reminds readers that human life is physical, earthbound, and shaped by divine power.
Divine Justice
The idea that the gods respond to human actions, especially arrogance or boundary crossing. The epic does not always present divine justice as simple or fair, but it does show that human beings cannot ignore the gods without consequence.
Ea
The clever god who warns Utnapishtim about the Flood. Ea's role shows that the gods do not always agree with one another and that mercy can exist within divine conflict.
Enkidu
Gilgamesh's equal, friend, and moral counterweight. He begins in the wilderness, becomes human through Shamhat and civilization, joins Gilgamesh in heroic action, and dies as a consequence of the heroes' overreach.
Enlil
A powerful god connected to the decision to destroy humanity in the Flood. Afterward, he grants immortality to Utnapishtim and his wife, making them exceptions to the normal human condition.
Epic
A long narrative work about heroic action, cultural values, and major questions about human life. Gilgamesh is often described as one of the oldest surviving literary epics.
Fame
The lasting reputation Gilgamesh seeks through heroic achievement. The epic asks whether fame can truly answer the problem of death.
Flood
The great destruction described by Utnapishtim. The Flood story places Gilgamesh's personal fear of death inside a larger vision of human fragility before divine power.
Friendship
The bond between Gilgamesh and Enkidu. Friendship humanizes Gilgamesh, gives him companionship, and later exposes him to grief when Enkidu dies.
Gilgamesh
King of Uruk, two-thirds divine and one-third human. He begins as a powerful but unjust ruler and gradually learns humility through friendship, loss, failure, and return.
Grief
The intense sorrow Gilgamesh feels after Enkidu's death. Grief changes the epic from a story of heroic fame into a search for wisdom about mortality.
Heroism
The pursuit of great deeds, courage, and public honor. Gilgamesh complicates heroism by showing that heroic action can be brave and morally troubling at the same time.
Hubris
Excessive pride or overconfidence. Gilgamesh and Enkidu show hubris when their victories lead them to insult divine powers and ignore limits.
Humbaba
The terrifying guardian of the Cedar Forest. Although he appears monstrous, he is also appointed by the gods, which makes his death morally complicated.
Immortality
Life without death. Gilgamesh seeks immortality after Enkidu dies, but the epic teaches that immortality is not available to ordinary human beings.
Ishtar
A powerful goddess who proposes marriage to Gilgamesh. His harsh rejection of her leads to the Bull of Heaven episode and helps set the tragedy in motion.
Kingship
The responsibility of ruling a city and protecting a people. Gilgamesh begins by abusing kingship, but the epic slowly teaches him that greatness requires wisdom and restraint.
Legacy
What remains after a person dies. The walls of Uruk, human craft, memory, and story become the epic's mortal answer to death.
Mortality
The condition of being subject to death. Mortality is the central human limit Gilgamesh must learn to accept.
Ninsun
Gilgamesh's wise mother, a goddess who interprets dreams and blesses the Cedar Forest journey. She represents counsel, care, and divine connection.
Scorpion Beings
Guardians at the mountain passage connected to the road of the sun. They mark Gilgamesh's movement beyond ordinary human space.
Serpent
The creature that steals the plant of rejuvenation while Gilgamesh bathes. The serpent's theft ends Gilgamesh's final hope of escaping age and decline.
Shamash
The sun god who supports Gilgamesh and Enkidu, especially during the Cedar Forest quest. His help complicates the question of whether divine support makes human action morally right.
Shamhat
The temple woman who brings Enkidu into human society. Through her, Enkidu gains human knowledge, food, clothing, language, and social identity, but he also loses his old bond with the animals.
Siduri
The tavern keeper at the edge of the world. She advises Gilgamesh to accept ordinary human life: food, clean clothing, work, love, and daily joy.
Sleep Test
Utnapishtim's test of Gilgamesh. If Gilgamesh cannot conquer sleep, he cannot conquer death; the failed test proves the limits of heroic will.
Tablet
A clay writing surface used in ancient Mesopotamia. The epic survives through fragments and versions preserved on tablets, reminding readers that literature itself is a form of memory.
Urshanabi
The boatman who helps Gilgamesh cross the Waters of Death and later returns with him toward Uruk. He functions as a guide between impossible desire and human return.
Uruk
Gilgamesh's city. Its walls frame the beginning and ending of the epic and symbolize human achievement, community, labor, and legacy.
Utnapishtim
The survivor of the Flood who was granted immortality by the gods. Gilgamesh seeks him for the secret of eternal life, but Utnapishtim teaches him that his case cannot be repeated.
Waters of Death
The dangerous boundary Gilgamesh crosses to reach Utnapishtim. The waters symbolize the distance between ordinary human life and the impossible answer Gilgamesh seeks.
Wilderness
The natural world outside the city. Enkidu begins there, and Gilgamesh later returns to a wild condition after grief drives him away from Uruk.
Wisdom
The hard-earned understanding Gilgamesh gains by failing to defeat death. Wisdom in the epic means accepting human limits while recognizing the value of friendship, work, community, and story.
